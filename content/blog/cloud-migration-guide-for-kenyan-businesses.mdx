Moving to the cloud is one of the highest-impact decisions a growing business in Kenya can make. Done well, it cuts hardware costs, improves uptime, and lets your team work securely from anywhere. Done badly, it causes downtime, surprise bills, and security gaps. This guide walks through a migration approach we use with clients across Nairobi and beyond.

## Why Kenyan businesses are moving to the cloud

On-premise servers are expensive to buy, power, cool, and maintain — and power reliability adds another layer of risk. Cloud platforms let you pay only for what you use, scale up during busy periods, and recover quickly from failures. For many local businesses, the cloud also makes it far easier to support hybrid and remote teams.

## The five phases of a safe migration

### 1. Assess

Start with an honest inventory: which applications, databases, and files you run, how they depend on each other, and how critical each one is. This tells you what can move first and what needs special care.

### 2. Plan

Choose a target — public cloud, private, or hybrid — based on cost, data-residency needs, and compliance. Map each workload to a migration strategy: rehost ("lift and shift"), re-platform, or rebuild.

### 3. Prepare

Set up secure networking, identity, and access controls **before** any data moves. Getting permissions and backups right up front prevents the most common migration incidents.

### 4. Migrate

Move in waves, starting with low-risk workloads. Validate each one before moving on. Keep the old system running until the new environment is proven.

### 5. Optimise

Once live, right-size your resources, turn off what you don't need, and set budgets and alerts so costs stay predictable.

## Avoiding the common pitfalls

- **Underestimating bandwidth.** Large data transfers take time — plan the cutover window carefully.
- **Skipping the cost model.** Model your monthly spend before committing, and set billing alerts.
- **Forgetting security.** The cloud is secure, but configuration is your responsibility. Enforce least-privilege access and encrypt data in transit and at rest.
