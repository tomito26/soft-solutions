Your network is the foundation everything else runs on — cloud apps, VoIP calls, card payments, video meetings, and backups all depend on it. When it's slow or unreliable, the whole business feels it: calls drop, files won't sync, and staff lose time to problems that have nothing to do with their actual work. A little planning up front avoids years of frustration. Here are the practices we build into every network we deliver across Kenya.

## Start with the right foundation

A network that performs well for years usually got the basics right on day one. Cutting corners on the foundation is the false economy that businesses pay for later.

### Structured cabling

Even in a wireless world, a proper wired backbone matters. Quality cabling, neatly terminated and clearly labelled, makes the network faster, easier to troubleshoot, and far simpler to expand later. When someone traces a fault, an organised patch panel turns an afternoon of guessing into a five-minute fix. Run enough cabling for where the business is heading, not just where it is today — pulling extra cable during the initial fit-out is cheap; doing it afterwards means disruption and higher cost.

### Business-grade equipment

Consumer routers and switches aren't built for sustained business load. They struggle under many simultaneous connections, lack the security and management features you need, and tend to fail sooner. Business-grade switches, routers, firewalls, and access points deliver better throughput, proper security controls, and the reliability that a busy office depends on. They also give you visibility — the ability to see what's happening on the network and manage it centrally rather than device by device.

## Design for reliability

Reliability isn't luck; it's designed in. A resilient network keeps working when individual parts don't.

- **Plan for redundancy.** A backup internet link — ideally from a different provider or technology — keeps you online when your primary connection goes down. For the systems you can't afford to lose, redundant hardware and automatic failover pay for themselves the first time they're needed.
- **Segment your network.** Separate guest Wi-Fi, staff devices, payment systems, and critical infrastructure onto different segments (VLANs). This contains problems: a visitor's infected laptop or a compromised device can't reach the systems that run your business.
- **Manage power.** Power dips and outages are a fact of life on many local grids. UPS units protect equipment from surges and give you a clean shutdown or ride-through during short outages, while surge protection guards against the spikes that quietly kill hardware over time.
- **Prioritise critical traffic.** Quality-of-service settings keep voice and video smooth even when the network is busy, so an important call doesn't stutter because someone started a large download.

## Build security in from day one

A network is one of the most common attack surfaces, and retrofitting security is far harder than building it in from the start. At minimum:

- Use a properly configured firewall — an out-of-the-box default is not a security policy.
- Change default passwords on every device, including switches, access points, and printers.
- Keep firmware updated so known vulnerabilities are closed.
- Use strong, separate credentials for admin access and guest Wi-Fi, and never share the same password across devices.
- Turn off services and ports you don't use; every one you leave open is a door you have to defend.

Good network security and good [cyber security](/cyber-security) practice go hand in hand — the network is where many attacks begin, so the two should be designed together.

## Plan for growth and visibility

Design with headroom so adding staff, a new department, or a second office doesn't mean starting over. Leave spare switch ports, size your internet connection with room to grow, and choose equipment that can scale with you.

Just as important, put monitoring in place — you can't fix what you can't see. Good monitoring tracks bandwidth, device health, and connection quality, and flags issues before your users notice them. When a problem does occur, the difference between "the internet is down" and knowing exactly which link or device failed is the difference between minutes and hours of downtime.

## Don't forget maintenance

A network isn't "set and forget." Regular reviews, firmware updates, and capacity checks keep performance high and security tight over time. Schedule periodic health checks, keep documentation current as things change, and review your setup whenever the business grows or moves. A small amount of routine maintenance prevents the large, disruptive failures that always seem to arrive at the worst moment.

## A quick pre-launch checklist

Before you consider a network finished, confirm you have:

- Structured, labelled cabling with room to expand.
- Business-grade, centrally manageable equipment.
- A backup internet link for business-critical connectivity.
- Network segmentation separating guest, staff, and critical systems.
- UPS and surge protection on key equipment.
- A configured firewall, changed default passwords, and updated firmware.
- Monitoring that alerts you to problems early.
- A maintenance schedule and up-to-date documentation.
